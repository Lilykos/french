## 1. Rules for Counting in French

Understanding how to count in French is essential for everyday situations like shopping, telling time, discussing dates, and more. The French number system has some unique features that differ from English, particularly for larger numbers.

### Numbers 1 - 20

Numbers from 1 to 20 are unique forms that need to be memorized individually. The first 16 numbers have distinct forms, while 17-19 follow a pattern based on 10 + units.

### Tens (20-100)

After 20, French numbers begin to follow more regular patterns, with some exceptions.

| Number | French    | Notes                                       |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------------------------|
| **20** | vingt     | Basic form                                  |
| **30** | trente    | Basic form                                  |
| **40** | quarante  | Basic form                                  |
| **50** | cinquante | Basic form                                  |
| **60** | soixante  | Basic form                                  |

For numbers between the tens, simply add the unit after the ten:
- **22**: vingt-deux
- **35**: trente-cinq
- **48**: quarante-huit
- **56**: cinquante-six
- **69**: soixante-neuf

**Special Case**: Numbers ending in 1 (except 81 and 91) use "et un" (and one):
- **21**: vingt **et** un
- **31**: trente **et** un
- **41**: quarante **et** un
- **51**: cinquante **et** un
- **61**: soixante **et** un

### Numbers 70-100: Standard French vs. Swiss French

French numbers become more complex from 70-99, with regional variations worth understanding.

#### Standard French (used in France, parts of Belgium, etc.)

| Number | French          | Construction                 |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| **70** | soixante-dix    | 60 + 10                      |
| **71** | soixante et onze| 60 + 11                      |
| **72** | soixante-douze  | 60 + 12                      |
| **80** | quatre-vingts   | 4 Ã— 20                       |
| **81** | quatre-vingt-un | 4 Ã— 20 + 1                   |
| **90** | quatre-vingt-dix| 4 Ã— 20 + 10                  |
| **99** | quatre-vingt-dix-neuf | 4 Ã— 20 + 19            |

#### Swiss French (used in Switzerland)

In Swiss French (and also in Belgian French), the number system is more logical:

| Number | Swiss French | Construction |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **70** | septante    | Direct form for 70 |
| **71** | septante et un | 70 + 1 |
| **80** | huitante (or quatre-vingts in some regions) | Direct form for 80 |
| **90** | nonante     | Direct form for 90 |
| **99** | nonante-neuf | 90 + 9 |

### Hundreds and Thousands

For hundreds and thousands, French follows these patterns:

| Number | French      | Notes                          |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------------|
| **100**| cent        | Basic form                     |
| **101**| cent un     | Note: no "et" with cent       |
| **200**| deux cents  | Note the plural "s" on "cents" |
| **300**| trois cents | Same plural pattern           |
| **1000**| mille      | Never takes a plural "s"      |
| **2000**| deux mille | No "s" added to mille        |

When combining with other numbers, the plural "s" on "cent" is dropped:
- **205**: deux cent cinq (not "deux cents cinq")
- **350**: trois cent cinquante (not "trois cents cinquante")

### Complex Numbers

For complex numbers combining hundreds, tens, and units:

- **732**: sept cent trente-deux
- **1990**: mille neuf cent quatre-vingt-dix (or mille neuf cent nonante in Swiss French)
- **2025**: deux mille vingt-cinq
- **1578**: mille cinq cent soixante-dix-huit (or mille cinq cent septante-huit in Swiss French)
